1: Speed VS Accuracy Trade-off

Start with a slow but accurate search in all directions and then to transition into a less accurate but much faster search.
Go wide first and narrow later.
This is not the only search strategy that works, but for Indie Games in particular this is a very reasonable one.
You have an exploration phase in the beginning where you try to figure out the best direction for you to go in‚Ä¶ We tried out a crap load of different ideas‚Ä¶ every protype is essentially a measurement‚Ä¶ to see if there's potential in this direction.
Next phase would be the pre-production, where you still want to do some active search to narrow in on the exact direction you want to go in.
At this stage Paul and I prototyped a very simple economy and combat system in unity while simultaneously doing some very active search and experiments on our art style.
In pre-production you are still searching just in a smaller radius.
Finally, in production you sacrifice your search accuracy for speed cause you got to finish your game someday.
For ‚ÄòThrone Fall‚Äô this meant rebuilding all of our systems from scratch. I rebuild the combat system, Paul rebuilt the economy system. We put it all together in a little blockout, and from there we just kept polishing‚Ä¶
I think it is important to note that you should never stop search completely you still want to do your occasional measurements.
You want to do your occasional play tests - you still want to find the deepest spot in the lake. So every now and then you should just give yourself the opportunity to correct course if necessary.

2. Local Minimum

Sometimes, in your initial testing, you may try a couple of prototypes and come to a conclusion you have found the best one. But how do you know there isn‚Äôt a even better solution that you haven‚Äôt tested.

Therefore, you must try make dramatic changes between prototypes to be able to ‚Äòsearch‚Äô further and be more confident that your final decision is the best.

DARE bigger jumps in your search tree now and then.
[Mark Brown - GMTK]
Make big changes.
We've already talked about iterating on a problem - but development time is limited, so if you're only ever changing things by tiny amounts - a 5% nerf here, a 6% buff there - you may never get to the right answer. Or you'll find out too late that your solution would never have worked.
Meier's advice is to "double it, or cut it in half".
With such dramatic changes, you'll very quickly see if the change has an effect - and if you go too far, you can always drop back down to readjust.

An opportunity to do this is game modes. Game modes allow you to keep the same mechanics, art etc. and simply change the objective.

Fortnite was initially meant to be some kind of ‚ÄòBase building tower defense‚Äô game - that's why you have all of these building mechanics in there.

5. Noisy Measurements

Have multiple play testers.

Also, if testing the game yourself, take some time between each test to reset your emotions etc. and get a more unbiased opinion.

6. Exploration Costs

The lack of exploration is far more expensive.

Make quick and dirty prototypes to rapidly iterate through design ideas and get more experience.

The cheaper your exploration, the more you can afford.

This may mean make a separate project to test a specific idea. Or perhaps it may be easier to use your existing project to be the foundation to test your idea.

Rule of thumb is - do whatever is fastest.

When two teams research separately and return with two different ideas, it may be difficult to decide on one.

I've really only come across one good solution for this problem so far: the two captains need to‚Ä¶ switch places and continue the search from the other Captain's position for a bit.
This does two fantastic things.
Firstly, it removes the emotional aspect cause now both captains have invested an equal amount of time into each idea.
Secondly, it brings a New Perspective to each of those search branches making those ideas only better and better - you will be surprised how much of a rational argument people can all of a sudden have.

Also, have captains for specific areas that have the final say.

For example, you can have one captain that always has the last word on art decisions and one captain that always has the final say in gameplay decisions.

Also, avoid having too many people in charge.

The decision-making process needs to be fast and having too many decision-makers leads to way too much friction.
